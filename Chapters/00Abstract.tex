The Tsetlin Machine (TM) is a logic-based machine learning algorithm, which incorporates finite-state automata and propositional logic into its structure. TM inference can be reduced to bitwise operations and integer arithmetic, making it a suitable target for hardware acceleration on resource-constrained platforms. An earlier project thesis \textcolor{red}{cite meg} showed that a custom clause evaluation instruction, added to a NEORV32 RISC-V processor through its Custom Functions Unit (CFU), accelerated inference time on the small NoisyXOR problem by a factor of $4.42$. This thesis investigates if a similar speedup will carry over to a more realistic workload like MNIST digit classification, where the trained model of approximately 3.81 MB is roughly $8000\times$ larger and far exceeds the on-chip memory of the processor.

A multi-class TM with 2000 clauses per class was trained on a host PC and deployed  on a Nexys A7 board featuring a Xilinx Artix-7 100T FPGA, with the model stored in off-chip DDR2 SDRAM and transferred from QSPI Flash at boot. Three inference implementations, a baseline software version and two CFU designs, were evaluated under two model load strategies and benchmarked on a fixed 200-image test set.

All configurations achieved a classification accuracy of approximately 97\%. One CFU design improved inference time by less than 1\%, while the other worsened it by less than 1\%, both of which are a stark contrast to the earlier result. The measurements show that at MNIST scale it is the movement of model data, rather than clause evaluation, that dominates inference time, accounting for around 91\% of the inference time. This means the performance bottleneck moves from computation to memory transfer for growing model sizes, limiting the possible benefits from processor-level clause acceleration.
